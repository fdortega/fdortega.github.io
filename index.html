Work

Banking on Customer Growth - Unsupervised Learning Project

In efforts to acquire more deposit customers, many banks offer tempting incentives or promotions. The next challenge for the bank is to actually retain the customers. Single product customers are the most at risk of switching banks. A key in customer retention is to develop a relationship with clients by serving as more than just a place to keep deposit accounts. This unsupervised learning project will cluster a sample of 5,000 customers and conduct an analysis of each cluster’s characteristics and their corresponding banking relationships. The objective is to leverage the insight from the analysis to develop more efficient marketing campaigns focused on deepening client relationships and improving client retention.

Predicting Prison Sentences - Supervised Learning Project

Washington DC is a city that in 2018 ranked among the top cities in the United States regarding arrests per capita. The city’s population demographic in terms of race is significantly disproportionate to the prison demographic. Questions related to equality exist at multiple steps along the incarceration process, from the initial police stop to the imposing of a sentence. This project focuses on the sentencing phase and analyzes a dataset that contains arrest records in Washington DC between 2010 and 2018. The primary objective of this supervised learning model will be to apply regression models to predict prison sentences for a person found guilty of a crime. The secondary objective is to determine whether race is a significant variable in the prediction of a sentence.

Eye Alert - Deep Learning Project

TexEn, a fictional energy provider is interested in energy consumption trends in the two largest cities in Texas. As they look to develop new price plans for service, they want to know whether energy consumption is stable year over year, or whether any trends in demand shifts exist. This project will analyze a dataset created by the U.S. Energy Information Administration for the timeframe including 2018 through April 2020. Data manipulation, normality, and nonparametric tests were utilized to ultimately determine if any significant differences exist in energy consumption in either Houston or Dallas.

About

Thank you for stopping by to learn a little bit about me. My name is Felix Ortega and I am a data scientist with a background in engineering and finance.

As a kid, I was known to tinker with things to figure out exactly how they worked. I ended up breaking a few of my sibling’s toys along the way, but they have since forgiven me for it…I think.

Many years later, that inquisitiveness still thrives. It is what drives me to continue to learn new skills and build upon my diverse work experience.

These days, instead of toys, I tinker with data. I analyze datasets to identify trends, detect correlations, and create predictive models. I am enthusiastic about the future, and how data science can help shape it.

When I am away from the office I enjoy spending time at the beach with family. I am a firm believer in work hard, play hard!
